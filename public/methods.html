Supplementary Methods

LYMPHOGUARD is a research prototype for real-time immune fitness monitoring during chemoradiation therapy. It combines pre-treatment risk stratification, wearable physiology, ALC trajectory modeling, and clinician-facing alerting.

Overview

The system is designed to bridge the gap between weekly complete blood count measurements. It continuously reviews wearable signals, patient check-ins, and lab results to identify immune deterioration early enough for clinical review, early labs, dosimetric assessment, or protocol-specific intervention.

Clinical Problem

Radiation-induced lymphopenia affects a substantial share of patients receiving thoracic chemoradiation. Published estimates suggest 40-70% of patients develop Grade 3 or higher lymphopenia, defined as ALC below 500 cells/uL. Severe lymphopenia is associated with reduced overall survival, higher infection risk, and impaired response to sequential immunotherapy.

Standard surveillance relies on weekly CBC lab draws. A clinically meaningful ALC decline can therefore remain undetected for several days. LYMPHOGUARD addresses this detection gap by using continuous physiological data as an early warning layer.

Scientific Rationale

  1. Thoracic radiation exposes circulating lymphocytes as blood passes through the treatment field. High confidence
  2. Large tumor volume, nodal burden, chemoradiation, age, and dosimetric exposure are plausible predictors of lymphopenia risk. High confidence
  3. Physiological deterioration can alter resting heart rate, HRV, oxygen saturation, activity, and sleep before lab confirmation. Moderate confidence
  4. Wearable data may provide actionable lead time when calibrated against real ALC measurements. Requires prospective validation

Model Architecture

The current architecture is intentionally modular, allowing each component to be replaced as paired ALC, dosimetric, and wearable data become available.

Component A: Pre-treatment Risk Stratifier

A Random Forest model estimates baseline lymphopenia risk from clinical features including stage, histology, treatment type, age, sex, and simulated gross tumor volume. The current public-data prototype uses survival as a surrogate target because paired ALC data are unavailable.

Component B: Physiological Deterioration Detector

A Gradient Boosting model reviews wearable-style features over trailing windows: resting heart rate, HR variability, oxygen saturation, respiratory trends, activity, sleep, and divergence patterns. The prototype was trained on MIMIC-III demo vital-sign windows as a proof of pipeline structure.

Component C: ALC Trajectory Model

A trajectory model forecasts week-by-week ALC with uncertainty bounds and is recalibrated when actual lab results are entered. In a validated deployment, this component would be personalized after early treatment labs and paired wearable observations.

Mathematical Framework

The following equations summarize the physiological signal layer and the ALC prediction interface.

HR-SpO2 Divergence Index

D(t) = ΔHR(t) / ΔSpO2(t), normalized over a 72h window

Deterioration Score

S(t) = sigmoid(β0 + β1*steps + β2*HR + β3*HRV + β4*SpO2 + β5*sleep)

ALC Trajectory Prediction

ALC(t) = f(S(t), ALC(t-1), EDIC, baseline)

Data Requirements

Clinical validation requires pre-treatment staging, GTV volume, histology, treatment plan, weekly ALC values, dosimetric features such as V5, V10, V20, mean heart dose and mean lung dose, plus daily wearable signals. These data would enable direct modeling of ALC nadir and false alarm rates rather than relying on surrogate labels.

Validation Plan

Stage 1: Retrospective Validation

A 200-500 patient historical thoracic CRT cohort would be used to train and evaluate the risk stratifier, trajectory model, and alert thresholds. The principal outputs would include AUC for Grade 3+ prediction, calibration of nadir timing, MAE of ALC forecasts, and identification of dosimetric drivers.

Stage 2: Prospective Shadow Validation

A 50-100 patient prospective study would run LYMPHOGUARD in shadow mode while standard weekly labs continue. Alerts would be recorded but not shown to clinicians. The analysis would determine whether wearable deterioration signals precede lab-confirmed Grade 3+ lymphopenia and by how many days.

Limitations

The current prototype has not been validated on paired wearable and ALC measurements. Model B uses ICU-derived data rather than ambulatory oncology wearables, Model C uses synthetic trajectory structure, and the public datasets do not include the strongest known dosimetric predictors. Alert thresholds are heuristic and false positive rates are unknown.

LYMPHOGUARD is a research prototype only. It is not cleared or approved for clinical decision-making and should be interpreted as a clinical aid requiring independent physician judgment.
